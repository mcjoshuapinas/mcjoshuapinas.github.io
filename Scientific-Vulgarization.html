Outdoor heatwave, June 22-25, 2026

Published by Joshua Piñas on July 3, 2026 in Paris.

Between June 22 and 25, 2026, metropolitan France experienced a period of high temperatures. This is a particular episode where temperatures remained high throughout the day, including at night (> 24 °C), breaking several records.

figure>

The solar radiation (horizontal) remained very intense (peaks of 800 W/m 2), directly impacting the walls of buildings. Some of this energy is reflected, while the rest is absorbed, stored and then transmitted by conduction inward. This transfer depends on the thermal properties of the materials and the building’s management.

When radiation hits glass surfaces, the energy enters almost instantly. It is then redistributed between indoor air, walls, furniture, and occupants, according to complex and non-linear physical laws.

How can I limit the thermal impact?

Because the building is a physical system, occupants can act on their surroundings to mitigate its effects:

  • Solar gain management: Using external blinds during the day helps limit incoming solar energy from glazing.
  • Natural cooling: Opening windows when the outside air is cooler than the inside air creates an airflow to release accumulated heat.
  • Cooling by evaporation: Watering the exterior walls and windows/balconies lowers surface temperature by evaporating water, thereby limiting heat transfer to the interior.
  • Use of fans: They move air and help sweat evaporate from the skin. However, their efficiency decreases beyond 35 °C, and their motor releases a slight residual heat in the room.
  • Behavioral adaptation: In case of persistent heat, it is advisable to occupy the coolest rooms, moisten bed linen, or adapt your diet (light meals, hydration water/salt/lemon juice).
  • Passive solutions: To avoid the use of portable air conditioners (energy-intensive and environmentally unfriendly), thermal renovation studies are recommended.

When all other measures fail, it is sometimes necessary to look for a cool or cooler place to spend the night.

Prevention for establishments open to the public

These recommendations can be applied to single-family homes and multi-family housing. For public buildings (schools, hospitals, etc.), specific prevention plans are essential to protect vulnerable people, such as children or the elderly, whose thermal regulation system (hypothalamus) is often less effective.

Finally, although we have focused on temperature and radiation, let’s not forget that humidity and wind speed also play a key role in our thermal comfort.
